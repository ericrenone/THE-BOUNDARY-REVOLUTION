### The Immediate Ones (Test Them Today)

**1. Josephson Diode Efficiency Traces the Majorana Phase Boundary**
Your lab has a nanowire junction. You apply a magnetic field and measure the asymmetry in current forward vs. backward (the "diode efficiency"). Plot efficiency η vs. magnetic field B. You should see:
- η ≈ 0 at low field (no topological phase)
- Sharp rise at B = B_c1 (topological phase begins)
- Maximum η inside the topological window
- Sharp drop at B = B_c2 (topological phase ends)
- Polarity reversal right at B_c2

If you see this, you've mapped the Majorana phase boundary without ever resolving an individual electron. **Most labs could run this test in three months.**

**2. The Rotating Zeeman Field Makes Topological Superconductors Controllable**
A rotating magnetic field at frequency Ω acts like a Floquet drive. When Ω hits the right frequency (where Ω = topological gap / ℏ), a trivial nanowire becomes topological. You're not applying a stronger field. You're rotating it. The rotation frequency controls topology.

Implication: **you don't need stronger magnets. You need cleverer field patterns.** This could enable topological computing at room temperature if the gap is large enough.

**3. Fractional Entropy in Kondo Systems Reveals Non-Abelian Statistics**
In two-channel Kondo systems, there's a fractional entropy per site of ln(√2). This is the quantum dimension of a Majorana zero mode. Apply a magnetic field. The entropy should decrease logarithmically as T → 0.

The rate of decrease dS/dB directly measures the "strength" of the non-abelian statistics. **This is thermodynamic spectroscopy of quantum dimensions.** It's cleaner than trying to physically braid Majorana modes.

**4. Valley-Majorana in WSe₂/Graphene Heterostructures**
Take monolayer graphene. Sandwich it between two WSe₂ layers with a specific twisted alignment. Cap with a magnetic oxide (RuO₂) and a superconductor.

The result: valley-Majorana modes. These are Majorana quasiparticles that also carry "valley" quantum number (K vs. K' valley in the band structure). They would show up as zero-bias peaks in tunneling that split when you measure K vs. K' valley separately.

**Why this matters:** valley is a new quantum number you can control with an electric gate. You get electrically-tunable Majorana braiding.

**5. Chiral Tellurium Nanowires Host Helical Majorana Modes**
Tellurium has intrinsic spin-orbit coupling because of its crystal structure. Recent experiments (June 2026) showed it has large, anisotropic g-factors. Pair it with a superconductor and you get Majorana modes.

But these aren't localized at the wire ends. They propagate helically along the wire axis, with pitch determined by the g-factor anisotropy.

**Why this matters:** helical Majorana modes are harder to accidentally create (no spurious zero modes), making this platform cleaner for quantum information.

**6. UTe₂ Corner-Localized Zero Modes (Second-Order Topological Superconductor)**

**7. Flexo-Zeeman Domain Walls as Majorana Nurseries**

**8. Altermagnetic Materials Replace the Three-Ingredient Recipe**
For 30 years, the recipe for Majorana modes was: semiconductor + spin-orbit coupling + Zeeman field + proximity superconductor.

Altermagnets break this recipe. They're magnetic materials where the magnetization alternates spatially (up, down, up, down...), so there's no net magnetic moment. Yet they generate k-dependent spin splitting—effectively a "Zeeman effect" in momentum space—and break symmetries the same way SOC does.

New recipe: altermagnetic material + proximity superconductor. That's it. No Zeeman field. No external SOC. The alternating magnetism does it all.

**Why this matters:** no stray magnetic fields. Compatible with sensitive superconductors and quantum devices. Materials are easier to engineer.

### The Three "Why Not Build These This Week?" Experiments

**Experiment 1: The Diode Efficiency Phase Map (Difficulty: Easy)**
You need: a nanowire Josephson junction (InAs/Al is standard), a magnet, a dilution refrigerator, standard electrical measurement setup.

What you do:
1. Cool the junction to 100 mK
2. Apply a magnetic field perpendicular to the nanowire, starting at 0 and going up to 5 Tesla
3. At each field strength, measure the Josephson current asymmetry (forward vs. backward critical current)
4. Plot the "diode efficiency" η(B) = (I_f - I_b)/(I_f + I_b)

What you expect to see:
- η ≈ 0 for B < B_c1 (trivial phase)
- Sharp rise and plateau for B_c1 < B < B_c2 (topological phase)
- Polarity reversal at B > B_c2 (trivial again)

Why it matters: this maps the Majorana phase boundary non-invasively. If you see the predicted shape, you've confirmed the framework's most basic prediction. If not, something fundamental needs revision.

Timeline: 2-4 weeks if equipment exists, 3-6 months if you need to assemble setup.

**Experiment 2: Rotating Zeeman Field Floquet Signature (Difficulty: Medium)**
You need: same junction as above, but with a rotating magnetic field generator (can be built from a spiral antenna or Helmholtz coils you rotate mechanically).

What you do:
1. Instead of a static field, apply a rotating field B(t) = B₀(cos Ωt, sin Ωt, 0)
2. Measure the zero-bias peak (tunneling conductance at V = 0) as you vary the rotation frequency Ω from 1 MHz to 1 GHz
3. The topological gap Δ_top can be estimated from other spectroscopy
4. Search for sharp features at resonance frequencies Ω_n = n × Δ_top/ℏ

What you expect to see:
- Weak zero-bias peak at low Ω (trivial phase)
- Sharp rise at Ω = Δ_top/ℏ (resonance with topological gap)
- Enhanced zero-bias peak, possibly 4π periodicity signature
- Return to weak peak at higher Ω

Why it matters: this would demonstrate that rotating magnetic fields can create topological phases from scratch, even in materials that aren't topological under static fields.

Timeline: 4-8 weeks for Floquet signature at resonance.

**Experiment 3: Entropy Response to Magnetic Field in Kondo System (Difficulty: Hard)**
You need: two-channel Kondo quantum dot, dilution refrigerator with sub-10 mK capability, calorimetry setup to measure specific heat.

What you do:
1. Create a two-channel Kondo system (this involves a lot of careful quantum dot tuning, but labs do this routinely)
2. Measure heat capacity C_V as a function of temperature and magnetic field
3. Extract the entropy S from ∫(C_V/T)dT down to base temperature

What you expect to see:
